Note on Market Structure

Kumar Aniket

UCL

04 April 2020, 13:22

Abstract

Market structure of an industry is the nature of the competition in an industry or the market segment. It is essentially describes how many firms operate within the industry and the way which which they compete.

This paper describes a simple framework that can be used to understand how the market structure of an industry can be predicted based on three parameters, i.e., sunk cost or fixed cost of developing a product, the potential size of the market and the consumer’s preference for variety.

 

Following @dixit1977monopolistic and @krugman1980scale, the market structure is considered endogenous, i.e., the structure of competition in the market is determined by some underlying fundamental characteristics. @dixit1977monopolistic’s insight was to show that market competition will emerge from two key factors.

  1. Increasing returns to scale.
  2. Consumer preference for variety.

Here is my parsimonious 3-parameter model that explains the key insight of this generation of Dixit-Stiglitz-Krugman market structure models that are currently being used across fields. The three parameters are:

  1. F sunk cost or fixed cost of developing a product.
  2. M potential size of the market
  3. ρ consumer’s preference for variety.

The first two parameters are related to the increasing returns to scale. The third one is related to the consumer’s preference for variety.1 F and M on their own can explain the supply side in all markets other than monopolistic competition. ρ is required to complete the model on the demand side in monopolistic competition.2 ρ was often assumed away in the past when supply side economics was in its ascendancy. Yet, we find that ρ is increasingly playing a pivotal role in a large number of theoretical and empirical paper as witnessed by the voluminous literature that has followed @melitz2003impact. The important question for you is whether monopolistic competition models are the relevant market structure model for climate change geo-engineering approaches.

Supply side

Increasing returns to scale

Take a very simple example of a production function with increasing returns to scale, i.e., fixed cost of setting up a plant but zero marginal cost of production.3 Let’s say the fixed cost of setting up production is F and there is no variable cost. F includes all sunk cost, i.e., innovation, acquiring project specific human capital and cost of establishing the brand.

Let’s say the market size is M. We will expand on M later in the section on consumer preference. For now, let’s freeze the demand side and think of M as simply the pounds that can be spent on buying goods in the market. It follows that

There would be $\frac{M}{F}$ firms in this market.

$\frac{M}{F}$ is the key parameter that determines the nature of competition in the market.

In an interesting paper, @krugman1987free show that $1<\frac{M}{F}<2$ in the jumbo jet industry. The fixed cost F of designing a jumbo jet is very high and the potential market size M is comparatively low. Increasing returns to scale restricts the number of firms in the market. Left to its own devices, there would be only 1 jumbo jet manufacturer in the market and other firms would have to exit. Yet, we have two, i.e., Boeing and Airbus.

@krugman1987free shows that highly scale oriented industries4 have externalities through their supply chain linkages. Each country thus has the incentive to capture these externalities. Hence, the market competition in an industry like the jumbo jet industry has become essentially a competition of strategic trade policy between the US and EU.

Thus, it is no longer a strategic game between Boeing and airbus. It becomes a strategic game between the US and Europe. Both US and Europe are keen to subsidise their own jumbo jet firms for two reasons.

  • These firms generate externalities that outweigh the subsidies and be captured locally.

  • Each country hopes that one day their firms will take over the market and if it does happen, then there are supernormal profits to be earned. We can illustrate this with the following example. Let’s say $\frac{M}{F}=1.6$. If someday Boeing exits the market due to some unforeseen circumstances, then Airbus would start earning 60% supernormal profit after its recovered F.

Varying M and F

There would be a large number of firms in the market segment if M is high and F is low. There would be a low number of firms in the market segment if M is low and F is relatively high. Let’s discuss the market structure for each range of $\frac{M}{F}$.

Case I: $\frac{M}{F}<1$: The market doesn’t exist. There are a lot of markets that don’t exist because the potential market size is smaller than the fixed cost of starting production. E.g., drugs that can be invented for niche diseases, but are not invented because the potential size of the market doesn’t cover fixed costs.

Case II: $1<\frac{M}{F}<2$: This is case of monopoly, but as you can see below, monopolies are not as simple as people presume they are. For $1<\frac{M}{F}<2$ there should be only one incumbent firm. The incumbent firm would earn supernormal rents $(\frac{M}{F}-1)$ due to the fact that there is only one firm in the market segment. It gives another challenger firm with deep-pockets incentive to enter the market segment and displace the incumbent. If the challenger enters, then it becomes a game of wits and deep-pockets between the challenger and the incumbent. @selten1978chain shows that this game becomes a game of credible threats of retaliation, i.e., reputation to follow through on threats.

  • If F is very high, then it is likely that there are a lot of supply chains and that implies that it becomes a game of strategic exports between national governments (@krugman1980scale).

Case III: $1<\frac{M}{F}<\underline{n}$, where $\underline{n}$ is small number greater than 1, say 5. This is the case of oligopoly. If there are a small number of firms in the market segment, the strategic game is likely to ensure with potential for collusion.5

  • If the firms play Cournot, i.e., compete with each other in quantities, there is always an implicit collusion equilibrium where the firms make super-normal profits.

  • If the firms play Bertrand, i.e., compete in prices, then firms make no profit over and above their costs.

    Cournot competition takes firms in the direction of monopoly. Bertrand competition takes the firms towards perfect competition. This is why a good competition policy always tries to ensure that firms compete in prices.6 Further, a good competition policy also has to ensure that market segments with fews firms remains competitive and firms are not able to find opportunities to implicitly collude through some extraneous means.

Case IV: $\frac{M}{F}\rightarrow\infty$: This is the case of perfect competition. There are a large number of infinitesimally small firms in the market with no market power. All firms compete and drive down the prices to marginal cost, which is zero because we assumed that there was no variable cost. Hence, all firms make negative profits and either the market collapses or a small number of firms find some extraneous means to erecting an artificial entry barrier, i.e., increase F through lobbying or corruption.

This is the reason why constant returns to scale is so critical for perfect competition.7 The moment there is increasing returns to scale, the market either collapses or you have an emergence of firms that can keep small challengers at bay through some artificial means.

Case V: $\underline{n}<\frac{M}{F}<\bar{n}$: This is the range in which monopolistic competition can potentially occur if certain other conditions on consumer preferences (see below) are satisfied. The essence of monopolistic competition is that number of firms $\frac{M}{F}$ is large enough so that they cannot strategically interact with each other, i.e., obtain the benefits from oligopoly. Yet, the $\frac{M}{F}$ is small enough so that market doesn’t collapse under the burden of perfect competition. As we will see below, monopolistic competition is a very distinctive kind of competition where consumer preference plays a critical role in determining the number of firms in the market segment.

Adding the variable cost adds more complications and makes its more realistic, but the basic insight about how increasing returns to scale impacts market structure is retained through it.

Consumer preferences

While consumer preferences are irrelevant if either $\frac{M}{F}<\underline{n}$ or $\frac{M}{F}\rightarrow\infty$, consumer preferences are of consequence in the intermediate range where monopolistic competition occurs. The key parameter that matters in this intermediate range is the consumer’s preference for variety, which determines the consumer’s willingness to pay more for variety. 8

There are $n=\frac{M}{F}$ firms in the market segment. Each firm produces a new variety by sinking in the cost F. The variety is appropriate in narrow market segments and n should be interpreted as a type of good if thinking about broader market segments. M is the size of the market, but as the analysis becomes more complicated because the size of the market itself is a function of ρ. This is intuitive. Apple iPhone increased the amount people spend on phones dramatically, hence increasing the size of M by inventing a new product. It drew in other firms and now the competition within a market segment is defined by the size of the market and cost of inventing a new variety (generation) of the smartphone, accounting of course for obsolesce. The simple M, F and ρ model captures the insight of the Dixit-Stiglitz-Krugman model.

A representative consumer’s utility derived from range a range of $n=\frac{M}{F}$ differentiated goods is given by $$U=\left[\sum_{i}^{\eta} x_i^{\rho}\right]^{\frac{1}{\rho}}$$ where i ∈ {1, n} represents the range of differentiated goods and ρ denotes consumers’ preferences for variety with.

  • If consumers have no taste for variety, i.e., ρ = 1 then it leads to a linear utility function U = x1 + x2 + … + xn. This implies that goods are are perfect substitutes for the consumer. If goods are perfect substitutes U = x1 + x2 + … + xn then consumers will go for the cheapest one, which potentially triggers a Bertrand competition amongst the producers and removes variety from the market.

    E.g., Market for wine in an area where people think all types of wines are perfect substitutes for each other. In this market, the cheapest wine ends up dominating the market. This is what happened to most commodities markets in the past in places people didn’t care about wheat or corn variety. Over time variety of wheat disappeared leaving us with cheapest variety to produce, the one invented by Norman Borlaug at the start of the Green revolution. A recent reversal of consumer preference, largely triggered by increasing prevalence of intolerance to gluten, has revived consumer preference for variety in wheat. As a result, products made from certain old varieties of wheat like Spelt and Khorasan are appearing back on the market shelves. Changes in ρ over time can collapse or revive a differentiated products market segment.

  • If consumer have a very strong taste for variety i.e., ρ → −∞ then it leads to a Leontief Utility function U = min [x1,x2,…,xn]. This means that consumers perceive goods as perfect complements production function. If goods are perfect complements, i.e., U = min [x1,x2,…,xn], then they have to be consumed in fixed proportion, irrespective of their price.

    Broad categories like food, housing and transport are an example of goods that are close to perfect complements are not that sensitive to prices. While prices may change the type of goods or type of housing people consume, there is complementarity between the broad categories.

  • Monopolistic competition occurs in the intermediate case where consumers have some taste for variety. That is if consumers perceive the goods as either imperfect substitutes (or imperfect complements).

What matters in monopolistic competition is that consumers are ready to pay more for variety. In our parsimonious setup, F represents the cost of creating a new variety. This captures the basic insight that creating a new variety requires innovation generating a new idea, which makes the production process exhibit increasing returns to scale. This innovation or new idea is also a non-rival good and as we will see below, it plays an important part in analysing any technological change associated with climate change. The fact that consumers’ are willing to pay variety implies the following four things.

  1. It gives a firm an incentive to create a new variety.

  2. The consumers pay so that firms can recover the cost of F and new varieties keep appearing till consumers are no longer willing to collectively bear the cost of creating another variety.

    Since we are abstracting away from variable costs, the only cost that matters is the total cost borne in creating variety. For n firms, this cost adds up to $\sum_i^n{F}=nF$. For all firms to break even, the size of the market needs to be greater than the total cost borne by the firms, i.e., nF < M. In equilibrium, the market would sustain $n=\frac{M}{F}$ varieties.

  3. The size of the market is a function of ρ. The potential market M is a function of consumer preference for variety represented by ρ, i.e., M = M(ρ).

  4. Each firm has no market power over the whole market.

In equilibrium, the number of firms or number of differentiated products $n=\frac{M(\rho)}{F}$ is ultimately determined by ρ and F.

Advertising

The moment firms can exert market power through some means, it starts becoming a strategic game between deep-pocket firms. Firms with deep-pockets spend on advertising or lobbying to influence ρ. Advertising increases ρ and the cost of advertising or lobbying increases the F required to be sunk in order to remain in the market. This decreases the number of firms in the market segment.

This explains why the soft-drinks market, even though it should not exhibit any increasing returns to scale, remains a duopoly. The incumbents with deep pockets have been able to turn the soft drinks market into an increasing return to scale production process, which in turn allows them to earn oligopoly rents. They are quick to buy up firms that threaten the duopoly.

  • Innocent drinks that had the potential to change the soft drinks market were bought up by Coco-cola in 2013.
  • The case of how Coke and Pepsi transformed the Indian soft drinks market in the 1980s is the other example that illustrates this. Before the entry of the deep pocket multinationals, the soft drinks market in India had a huge variety. Within years of Coke and Pepsi entering, the same market became a duopoly with all the other firms being driven out. The consumers’ preference for variety did not change suddenly, yet, the range of variety offered to them decreased through an implicitly collusive strategy of the two firms.

The upshot is that F can either be exogenously high because of technology or can be increased if some deep-pocket players within the market want to reduce competition in the market. Whether that strategy succeeds or not largely depends on the competition policy that is in place.

Demand Side

The geo-engineering product described in the paper sounds like a pure public good. It is both non-rival and non-excludable. Any public good provision is in essence an coordination game.

Let’s think of a street with several houses. There is no street light on the street and the local authorities refuse to put up the street light. The street light, in this case, is pure public good because the light from the street light is non-rival and non-excludable. Each house can choose to either contribute towards the street light or not contribute. Hence, it becomes a game between the households living on the street on whether to contribute or not and if they do, how much do they choose to contribute. There are two possible case and I list the Nash equilibria in each case.

  1. Private provision of public good: One participant feels so strongly that they are ready to self provide it and have the resources to do so. They have such a strong preference for the street light that they are ready to internalise the cost of paying for the public good on their own.9 This is essentially a person with ρ → −∞ who feels that the street light is a pure complement to their other consumption goods. In climate change movement one could think of Greta Thurberg or a dedicated climate change NGO as having ρ → −∞.

    This is also a unique Nash equilibrium because knowing that there is someone on the street with a strong preference for street light, it is optimal for all the other household not to contribute.

  2. Either no one participant feels so strongly about the street light or the ones that do do not have the resources to contribute the full amount. In this case, there is a good outcome that is not an equilibrium and there is a bad outcome that is an equilibrium.

    • Good outcome: There is an outcome where all houses contribute towards the street light and benefit from the street light. This is not a Nash equilibrium because if everyone else is contributing, it is optimal for a household to deviate and not contribute.

    • Bad equilibrium: No one contributes to the public good and the public good is not provided. No household has an incentive to deviate an contribute if no one else is contributing. This is the unique Nash equilibrium in this case.

If there is no private provision of public good, pure public goods require explicit coordination from the government so that the good outcome can be attained. Alternatively, technology can be used to make good excludable and create a private market from it.

Excludability is key to charging a price. Autoroutes in France is an example of using technology to change the nature of the good and making it excludable in order to create a private market for what is essentially a public good. Yet, this private market competes with public goods, i.e., other roads in France. What can be charged for this kind of public good depends on how non-rival the non-excludable alternatives are and consumer’s perceive substitutability between the two. In the case of autoroutes, the other competing goods are the highways that run along the autoroutes. If they are substitutes, no one would pay for autoroutes. The autoroutes system has been specifically designed in such a way that local roads are not a substitute for the autoroutes.

If having a healthy global environment is considered critical, then that means it complements other consumer goods. That implies that ρ → −∞ for these broad categories of goods, ie, environment and other consumer goods. If a country has a very high ρ, they would privately provide the public good. This seems highly unlikely in the current circumstances.

With ρ → 1, the bad equilibrium is inevitable in the public goods game. The ρ the various governments across the world have and how to change it is key debate about using increasing returns to scale technology in solving the climate change policy.10

Upshot

I suspect a range of extremely high F climate change policies are stuck in the bad equilibrium in spite of having a potential $\frac{M}{F}>1$ because the M is price governments have to pay and no one wants to pay it because of the coordination failure in the public goods game.

Further, there is a debate about whether to provide it as a public good or through market based measures. If geo-engineering is going to compete with all the market oriented solutions then it is important to pin down the efficacy of geo-engineering in terms of climate change outcome variables and comparing it to other solutions. That requires pinning down F and some proxy for M.

@krugman1987free shows partly how the bad equilibrium trap can be escaped. A highly scale oriented industry with a very high F is able to have two players in spite of the fact that $1<\frac{M}{F}<2$ because the increasing returns to scale offers some externalities that countries can capture and hence subsidise the high F.

Role of Financial markets

There is also an interesting story of how to finance a big F for increasing returns to scale that have public goods attributes. @de1999development makes the point that (laissez-faire) decentralised banking system underinvests and under transmits expertise. This is especially true if either F is very high or the planning horizon is very long. @de1999development thus argues for a development bank. There may be some merit to this idea for high F climate change mitigation technology projects that have public goods characteristics.
