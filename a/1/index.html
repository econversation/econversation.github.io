Mallick and Nabin (2018) show that access to microfinance is higher in villages where there is a higher proportion of land irrigated using electricity and the villages that are closer to the main marketplace of the region. They thus show that that access to microfinance is determined by factors that determine the village level productivity of the entrepreneurial projects.

Mallick and Nabin (2018) examine the program location choice of the MFIs using village level survey data from 156 randomly selected villages from districts of Kurigram, Rangpur and Nilphamari in Bangladesh.

They find that the proportion of people MFI’s lent to in a particular villages decreases with the distance from the marketplaces and increases with the percentage of land irrigated using electricity.

Mallick and Nabin (2018) show that the coverage of a village increase with electrification of agriculture and distance from the local market or district headquarter.

Reading Notes

…

mission drift: trade-off between the serving the poor and cost effectiveness of microfinance institutions.

much useful information gets lost about the local characteristics due to data aggregation at the higher level. only micro-level data can uncover the specific nature and reasons for the “mission drift” at the local level.

NGO coverage:

  • decreases with distance of village from main market place (NGO branch office)
    • increases with localised marketing opportunities
  • increases with good physical infrastructure (distance from paved road)
    • adoption of modern irrigation methods
